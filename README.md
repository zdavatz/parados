## Share

Every game has a **Share on WhatsApp** button (WhatsApp green `#25D366`) placed to the right of the game title. It opens the OS share sheet (`navigator.share`) with a pre-filled message containing the current game URL plus iOS App Store and Google Play links to the Parados app — the user picks WhatsApp from the share sheet and the message is handed over to it natively. Older browsers without the Web Share API fall back to opening `https://wa.me/?text=…` in a new tab. **Android fix (2026-05-12):** prior versions opened `wa.me` directly, which then redirected to `whatsapp://send/` inside the Android WebView — WebView doesn't dispatch app URL schemes to the OS, so it errored with `ERR_UNKNOWN_URL_SCHEME`. The `navigator.share` short-circuit bypasses the redirect entirely. When the game is played inside the iOS/Android app the page is loaded from a local `file://` path, so the share message rewrites the URL to the public `https://game.ywesee.com/parados/<filename>` so recipients always get a working link. **DUK / TIK / kangaroo share messages are localized per variant** — DE "Spiele DUK — Das ungeduldige Känguru — Stufe X: …", EN "Play TIK — The Impatient Kangaroo — Level X: …", plus JP / CN / UA each in their own language.

## Direct-entry links (URL hash anchors)

`divided_loyalties.html` (+ `_en`) and `kangaroo.html` (+ 4 language variants) accept a URL hash that auto-launches a specific starting position. Setup is skipped on match and the H1 inline subtitle reads e.g. `DIVIDED LOYALTIES — Octopus`. The Share-on-WhatsApp button appends the active position's slug to the link it sends, so recipients land directly on the same position.

- **Divided Loyalties:** `#up-the-stairs`, `#octopus`, `#wind-wheel`, `#gentle-kiss`, `#utopia`, `#e-m-3-w`, `#pyramid`, `#horseshoes`, `#swisscross`, `#picasso`, `#almostadiamond`, `#frankenstein` (+ numeric `#1`–`#12`) — same slugs work for `divided_loyalties.html` and `divided_loyalties_en.html`
- **DUK / TIK kangaroo:** `#first-steps`, `#the-block`, `#shifting`, `#staircase`, `#scattered`, `#slant`, `#the-cross` — slugs are identical across all 5 language variants (DE/EN/JP/CN/UA), so `kangaroo_jp.html#the-cross` and `kangaroo_en.html#the-cross` land on the same level. Numeric `#1`–`#7` works as shorthand; trailing `b` selects start variant B (e.g. `#the-crossb`).

## Games

- **DUK — The Impatient Kangaroo** — The plan: to create a 21st century successor to the super-hit solo puzzle game Rushhour. Many players think that hopping through the outback collecting goodies is more fun than trying to shove your way through traffic? Another advantage — thanks to the program, there's all kinds of different ways to play:-) Three difficulty tiers of handcrafted puzzles (Beginner → Intermediate → Advanced) plus "Take a Chance" random mode with three placement zones: Center 6×6 (12 dishes), Inner 8×8 (20 dishes), and Full board (34+ dishes) for endless replay — with color-balance validation ensuring green and blue dishes are well-distributed across the board. The rules modal also reminds players to click "Neues Spiel" if a board side ever ends up with 3 or more excess of one color, as a quick fallback. A big advantage over Rushhour: Beginner and Advanced puzzles offer two different starting positions (A/B) — same board, different challenge! Intermediate puzzles have a single fixed starting position. Rules button is prominently placed at the very top of the page (above the title) for easy access. **Dish move — double-click shortcut:** double-click (or double-tap on mobile) the dish you want to move — that puts it directly into move mode with the dish pre-selected; a single click on the target square then finishes the move. The "🖐️ Schale verschieben" button remains as a fallback. **Replay mode:** Load any exported CSV game log to watch the entire game replayed move by move — with play/pause, step forward/back, speed control, and visual highlights showing each dish move and jump. The initial board state is automatically reconstructed from the CSV data.

- **Rainbow Blackjack** — Colorful 21! Two players build 6 colored towers, trying to get as close to 21 as possible — like Blackjack, but with colored stones. The key twist: stones follow the color wheel (Red—Orange—Yellow—Green—Blue—Purple) — each stone can go in its own tower or the two neighboring towers. So Player 1's red stones can reach the purple and orange towers, while Player 2's green stones can reach yellow and blue. Both players compete over every tower! This game is easier to play than to describe:-) Arrange your stones in a grid — they stay face-down and hidden from your opponent, so memorize your positions! Each round, both players pick a row and announce, then alternate placing 1 stone at a time — announce just enough to keep your opponent guessing. Gray jokers add a devious twist...

- **MAKA LAINA** — It's the first turn and the battle is on! No time to get warmed up in MakaLaina:-) You need to be planning from the get go, evolving your long term strategy — but staying flexible. After each collection, a new disc is drawn from the bag — and YOU choose where to place it in your line of sight. Use this strategically to block your opponent! The disc can't be collected immediately (it's in your LOS), keeping the board count stable for the first 24 turns. After the bag empties, the tempo rises as discs disappear — and every decision carries more weight. Stuck with no valid moves? Your opponent cruelly picks which of your collected discs to sacrifice — then you reposition your collector. Or relinquish your turn — but only 2 times per game! Once you've used them up, you're forced to sacrifice when stuck — keeping the drama alive until the very last move. Secondary colors gain strategic importance as sacrifice buffers. The endgame is where it all comes together...
  - [Play](https://game.ywesee.com/parados/makalaina.html) | [Remote](https://game.ywesee.com/parados/makalaina_remote.html)
  - **Fair opening (Walter, 2026-05-13):** 24 random discs are dropped onto the board first. Then **Player 1 places both collectors** — they see the full disc layout and pick the two squares freely. Once both are down, **Player 2 picks which colour they want to play** (the better-positioned one, obviously) — Player 1 plays the other and takes the first turn. This neutralises the opening unfairness Walter was wrestling with on the old curated starts: the collector-placer has to commit to two positions before the colour-picker chooses, so any imbalance hurts whichever colour P1 ends up with. **No more curated starting positions** — the 10 hand-tuned layouts and their CSV file were removed; fairness now lives in the opening sequence, not in the disc layout. Color-majority scoring (most colors won wins; tiebreaker: highest single-color score). Leader indicators and winner announcement overlay. At game end, the status bar replaces "GAME OVER" with a persistent winner summary (e.g. "Black wins! 4–2") so players don't need to count colors themselves. Color-aware scoring table: each color name displayed in its actual color, worthless boxes in the disc's own color. CSV export available at any time during play — saves full move history with scores, flags, collector positions, and disc counts. Move history replay at game end — step through every move of the game with navigation controls. Both local and remote versions supported (remote: host = P1 places collectors, joiner = P2 picks colour via PeerJS). **↶ Undo move:** every action that ends a turn (collection, draft placement, sacrifice move, relinquish) snapshots full game state — click "↶ Undo move" to roll back the last action, click again to keep undoing back to the game-start snapshot. The button auto-disables at the start of the game and once a winner is declared. **Custom-position editor (local only):** the setup panel has an "✏️ Editor — Custom Position" button that opens an in-page 12×12 painter. Pick a brush — Red/Blue/Yellow 1–6 (primary discs), Green/Orange/Purple (secondary discs), or eraser — then click cells to compose a disc layout. Collectors are **not** painted in the editor — Player 1 places them live at game start just like in Random mode. "💾 Save" stores the layout in your browser (localStorage) and lists it on the setup screen with ★ Play / ✏️ Edit / 🗑 Delete buttons. "▶ Test play" launches the current paint without saving. "📋 Export CSV" emits a block of `position_id,element,x,y,color,number` rows (disc rows only — collectors live, not stored). "📤 Share via WhatsApp" attaches the CSV to a WhatsApp message via the OS share sheet (file-only — same Android `ERR_UNKNOWN_URL_SCHEME` and "wrong-text" lessons learned from Divided Loyalties). **Disc colour chooser (Walter, 2026-05-17):** the editor has a collapsible "🎨 Disc colours — pick your own" panel with a colour picker per disc colour (red/blue/yellow/green/orange/purple) plus "↺ Reset to defaults". Walter's choice is saved per device and applies everywhere in MAKA LAINA — game board, score table and editor all draw from the same palette (this also fixed an old mismatch where the board used bright raw CSS colours while the score table used softer shades). It's a local display preference, not part of any saved position; remote games keep the default palette.

## CSV Export

All games with CSV export save files both locally (browser download) and to the server's `csv/` directory. Server-side filenames follow the format `gamename_HHMM_dd.mm.yyyy.csv`. A Python CGI script (`cgi-bin/save_csv.py`) handles the server-side storage.

- **Frankenstein — Where's that green elbow?** — This is even shorter and sweeter than Rainbow. For 1–4 players, it's a "frankly memorable" game (you'll get the pun when you play it). Like most of its colleagues here at Think Ahead, it is so much easier to play online. Age recommendation — 7 years and up. Don't be surprised if the youngest player wins:-)) Each body part color (green, orange, purple, turquoise) has a unique character with real personality — green has wild spiky hair and a crazed bloodshot stare with a stitched scar over one eye and teeth-baring grimace, orange has dense curly hair and an unhinged asymmetric grin with a temple screw, purple sports a mohawk with a stitched scar across the entire face, fangs and a forked snake tongue, blue has a Frankenstein flat-top with sunken dark-ringed eyes, jagged teeth, extra cheek stitches and neck bolts. Ears are split between upper and lower head halves — mix-and-match for hilarious creations with mismatched ears! Head halves connect seamlessly. Anatomical thighs tapering from wide hip to knee with a kneecap arc, and lower legs with a calf muscle bulge tapering to thin ankles are clearly distinguishable from thin straight arms; spread-finger hands and wide flat feet with toe bumps are unmistakable. Clean borderless body tiles for a polished look. **Three build modes:** Classic (torso → outward), Reverse (extremities → inward), or Mixed (one player each direction!) — Reverse starts with 1 hand, 1 foot and 1 upper head pre-placed (instead of torso) — all 4 torso parts go in the deck and you build inward! In Mixed mode, one player builds outward while the other builds inward — a Walter Prossnitz original twist that dramatically increases replayability!

- **Divided Loyalties** — Many turns are offensive AND defensive and each one may have long term consequences! It's connect 4, but with 6 colours. Your colour is always loyal to you, your opponent's never is... and the other 4? Sometimes they are, and sometimes they aren't. Tiles can even be loyal in one direction, AND disloyal in another! Not for the faint of heart.... **Brücke setzen — Doppelklick auf erste + letzte Platte:** after you place a stone that completes a 3- or 4-in-a-row of loyal colors (no two adjacent same colors), double-click (or double-tap) the first stone of the row, then double-click the last stone — the bridge is drawn. A second double-click on the already-selected stone deselects. The first-selected stone gets a cyan outline so you can see which endpoint you've picked. Per Walter: on-board bridges are not automatic — the player decides which row to bridge and when. **Exception:** if you place on a placement dot (off-board), the bridge that justified the placement is drawn for you automatically — off-board placements are only legal if they complete a bridge in the same turn anyway. **A 3-bridge can no longer be extended to a 4-bridge (Walter, 2026-05-11):** once you've committed to a 3-bridge, you can't later add a fourth stone in line and form a 4-bridge over the same row. Walter's wording: "if you place a 3, your opponent doesn't have to worry, because you can't make it into a 4 — *Lauter Gemeinheiten in diesem Spiel!*" Strategic intent: deciding when to "lock in" a 3 vs holding out for a 4 is one of the game's core tensions. (If a 3-bridge gets *cut* by a red bridge, this restriction lifts — the cut bridge is destroyed in scoring terms and a 4-bridge can replace it.) Bridge lines are now thinner (normal bridges = stroke 4, cut bridges still stroke 6 with the dashed gray look Walter liked) so they read as crisp lines rather than thick bars. **Stone on a dot covers it cleanly:** when you place a stone on a placement dot, the dot disappears under the stone and the stone centers normally in the cell (was: dot and stone shared the cell side-by-side, both visibly off-center). **↶ Zug zurück + Spiel speichern (Walter, 2026-05-11):** every user action (placement, bridge, end turn) snapshots full game state to an undo stack — click "↶ Zug zurück" to undo the last action, click again to keep undoing back to the start. The current game also auto-saves to your browser, so closing and reopening the page shows a "▶ Letztes Spiel fortsetzen" button at the top of the setup screen. Manual "💾 Sichern" downloads a `.json` file you can keep or share; "📂 Laden" reads one back in; "📤 Per WhatsApp senden" attaches the saved game to a WhatsApp message via the OS share sheet. If the OS doesn't offer a files-share sheet, the fallback just downloads the `.json` and shows a note — the game stays on screen (was: the old fallback redirected to `wa.me` and made it look as if the game had vanished, Walter 2026-05-11). **Scoring split into two counters:** "Brücken" (3-bridge = 1pt, 4-bridge = 2pts) and "Regionen" (+1pt for each player's *first* bridge whose *inner* cell — the ones fully under the line, excluding the two endpoints — falls inside a region/board like S1 or G4). Both counters are shown side by side per player; players add them up themselves. **Cut bridges:** a red bridge that cuts a scoring bridge wipes both its bridge-points and its region-point — but the cut bridge remains physically in place (gray, dashed) and keeps blocking anything else from being built across it. New scoring bridges and new red bridges alike are rejected if their line would cross a cut bridge. Diagonal-bridge tie-breaker is planned but not yet implemented. **Dots light up like Kangy's landing spots:** select a color and every off-board placement dot where that color would complete a bridge starts pulsing teal-green, so you see all your bridge-completion options at a glance. Off-board placements are now rejected upfront if they wouldn't form a bridge — no more silent "stone placed then undone at end of turn". **Drei Sätze (three batches):** stones don't all arrive at once — each player gets 12 stones at a time across 3 batches (Blau: 4 Blau, 3 Lila, 2 Grün, 2 Rot, 1 Orange / Gelb: 4 Gelb, 3 Orange, 2 Grün, 2 Rot, 1 Lila). When the inventory empties, the next batch arrives automatically. Same total (36 each) as before, but the inflection points around moves 12 and 24 introduce new mid-game tactics and roughly double the depth of the endgame. The "Satz 1/3 → 2/3 → 3/3 (letzter)" indicator under the player turn label tells you where you are. **Inventory labels:** every slot now shows the colored swatch, the count, and the German color name underneath — so it's clear which "12" or "9" belongs to which color. **12 starting positions** in two game-length groups — a **4-round set (1–6)** and a **3-round set (7–12)**, contiguous so the split stays clean: 1 Up the Stairs, 2 Octopus, 3 Wind wheel, 4 Gentle kiss, 5 Utopia, 6 E M 3 W (15×15 — four 2×2 corners + four letter-shaped polyominoes spelling E/M/3/W around a 3×3 center), 7 Pyramid (17-wide stepped-pyramid silhouette), 8 Horseshoes, 9 SwissCross, 10 Picasso, 11 AlmostADiamond, 12 Frankenstein. (The earlier standalone "Swiss Cross", slug `#swiss-cross`, was retired 2026-05-16 — superseded by SwissCross from the 3-round set.) Non-square grids up to 17 columns wide with manual placement dots. Selectable at game start via setup overlay and re-openable via "Neues Spiel" button — the picker shows full-width section headers grouping the built-ins into **4-Runden-Spiel** (ids 1–6) and **3-Runden-Spiel** (ids 7–12), then **Eigene Stellungen** before any custom positions (English variant: *4-Round Game / 3-Round Game / Custom Positions*). The 4/3 boundary is a design convention, not stored in the CSV — a `FOUR_ROUND_COUNT` constant in `showSetup()` marks it. Positions are loaded from [`divided_loyalties_starting_positions.csv`](divided_loyalties_starting_positions.csv) at runtime (one CSV row per grid row, each cell its own column c0…c16); the same CSV is also embedded inside the HTML so the bundled iOS/Android/Rust apps work even when the WebView can't `fetch()` the external file. Edit the CSV to correct distances/shapes, sync the embedded copy in `divided_loyalties.html`, and reload — no other code change needed. **Position editor:** every position now has an "✏" button that opens an in-page editor (built-ins as a fresh copy, custom positions in-place). A "➕ Neue Stellung erstellen" button at the bottom of the setup overlay opens a blank 17×17 editor with **independent Breite/Höhe dropdowns** (9–25 each) — non-square positions like Walter's planned Oktopus (15×9) work end-to-end and the play grid renders rectangular accordingly (no wasted square padding). Resizing preserves painted cells. The brush bar offers S=3×3 dark, W=Pyramide mid, G=3×4 light (with numbered variants S1–S5/W1–W5/G1–G4 for separate boards), a **• Punkt** brush for marking off-board placement squares, plus an eraser. The editor grid sits on a white background; empty cells are blank with only a thin gray hairline border so Walter can see what he's painting on, and dots only appear where he paints them with the • Punkt brush. **Three ways to save / share:** "💾 Speichern" stores the position in your browser (localStorage) — it appears in the setup list with ★ on next open and only on your device. "📤 Per WhatsApp senden" exports the position as a `.csv` attachment via the OS share sheet — pick WhatsApp and the CSV file travels as a real attachment to the recipient. **Android fix (Walter, 2026-05-11):** the share call now passes only the file + a short title, no descriptive text body. Previously Walter reported "wenn ich Picasso sende, kommt Frankenstein!" — Android-WhatsApp was dropping the CSV attachment and sending only the (sometimes stale) text from a previous share draft. With no text body in the share, WhatsApp has no text-only path to fall back to and either attaches the file or fails cleanly (in which case the local download + manual-attach fallback kicks in). "📋 Als CSV exportieren" generates a CSV block in the same format as the existing positions file, ready to paste into `divided_loyalties_starting_positions.csv` so the position ships to all players via git. Saved custom positions also show a small 📤 button next to ✏ in the setup overlay, so any saved position can be sent again with one click. Grid size is computed dynamically per position from the CSV's max x/y (+2 cells of margin for out-of-board stone placement, per game rules). Board pieces are now all gray in three shades so the stones carry all the color (per Walter: "the whole game revolves around the color relationships"): **S** 3×3 darkest, **W** pyramid (3+2+1) middle, **G** 3×4 lightest — frames softened to match (no more high-contrast white-on-black). Each unique token (S1, S2, W1…) is its own separate board — so two 3×3s that touch stay two separately-scored regions with a visible seam between them. Background is a muted forest green distinct from the green stone color. Stone palette tuned for balance: orange and violet brighter, green darker (red remains saturated as the "evil" defensive color); stones now nearly fill their cells. **Bridge geometry:** bridges end at the cell edge (~2px clear of the outer stones, never overlapping them) and are drawn in slightly darker shades than the stones for legibility — yellow stays in the pure-yellow band so it doesn't drift toward orange. Bridges render **on top** of the stones (z-index 20 vs tile z-index 15), so a freshly-drawn bridge always lays over the row of stones it connects instead of disappearing behind them. **Off-board placement dots — manual:** the play grid no longer auto-shows a dot on every off-board cell. Instead, the position designer paints dots explicitly with a new **• Punkt** brush in the editor, and the play grid renders only those dots — the rest of the area is just plain white. Walter's rationale: "I will place them at the right spots. The rest of the area is unnecessary so can just be left white." The rule is unchanged: stones may go on a dot cell only if they complete a bridge in the same turn (Andres Kuusk: "an excellent rule"). Dots are stored alongside boards on each position (CSV cell value `o` = dot, `.` = empty), travel through the WhatsApp share / CSV export round-trip, and are saved with custom positions in localStorage.
